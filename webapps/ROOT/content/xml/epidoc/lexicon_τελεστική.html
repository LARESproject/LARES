Morphology

Morphology

Word formed by two elements. The verb τελέω, 1. to finish, complete, fulfill; 2. To pay; 3. To celebrate, perform, to initiate in the mysteries, to endow a thing with potency, consecrate it; and the agjectival suffix -ικός, ική.

The verb comes from the substantive τέλος, 1. end, realization; 2. culmination, perfection; 3. limit, conclusion; 4. Office, function; 5. Tribute, tax; 6. Offerings, sacrifices, ceremonies, rites, festivals, mysteries; 7. Military division, detachement. (Montanari 2015: pp. 2099-2100)

Etymology

Etymology

Τέλος has, according to scholars, two possible roots: the ancient root *kᵂel- “to turn”, that of τέλομαι or the root *tel- “to lift, bear”. According to Chantraine τέλος could derive from the extended root *tel-ₔ₂- “to lift”, that of τέλλω. (Chantraine 1999: p. 1103) According to Waanders instead of *tel-ₔ₂- “to lift, to hold up”, τέλος derives from *tel-ₔ₁-, “to carry out, to perform”. with the metaphorical meaning attached to it at an early date. Thus, although τελέω is generally considered by the ancients as well as by modern scholars, to have derived from τέλος, τελέω could be the primary verb and the noun τέλος would consequently derive from it. The verb would come from *τέλεμι, derived from *tel-ₔ₁-mi (Waanders 1983, p. 18).

According to Waanders: “the earliest attested meanings are '(full) performance, completion', 'realization', 'task', to which the other meanings can be related in a coherent way”. As a corroboration of this etymology, Waanders proposes to interpret the Mycenaean forms te-re-ta as τελε(ς)τᾱ́ς “a high-ranking person” (Waanders 1983: p. 20). In this precise sense the term is attested in an inscription of VI-V century BCE from Olympia, where the dialectal form τελεστά means “magistrate” (; Ballériaux 1990, p. 36).

Testimonies

Testimonies

1) The term τελεστική is first attested in IV century BCE, used by Plato only two times, both in .

In the first passage Plato is listing the different stages of human condition and at the middle, in 5th position we can find “the life of a prophet or someone who conducts mystic rites” (πέμπτην μαντικὸν βίον ἤ τινα τελεστικὸν ἕξουσαν). It is the very first time this expression is used by Plato and the scholars’ interpretation has not been so straightforward. Omer Ballériaux (1990, p. 35) states “Les traducteurs du Phèdre se montrent, à cet endroit, bien hésitants.” Generally, it is interpreted as something regarding mystic rites and mystery cults.

In the second passage (265b) Plato says that telestic madness comes from Dionysus. However there’s still no explanation of the term. To explain this telestic mania (μανία) Ballériaux (1990, p. 36) turns to the passage , where Plato writes about a mania without naming it, and by exclusion Ballériaux believes that it is the telestic one:

τό τε ὄνομα τοῦ ὀνόματος ἔργον τ’ ἔργου, τόσῳ κάλλιον μαρτυροῦσιν οἱ παλαιοὶ μανίαν σωφροσύνης τὴν ἐκ θεοῦ τῆς παρ’ ἀνθρώπων γιγνομένης. ἀλλὰ μὴν νόσων γε καὶ πόνων τῶν μεγίστων, ἃ δὴ παλαιῶν ἐκ μηνιμάτων ποθὲν ἔν τισι τῶν γενῶν ἡ μανί ἐγγενομένη καὶ προφητεύσασα, οἷς ἔδει ἀπαλλαγὴν ηὕρετο, καταφυγοῦσα πρὸς θεῶν εὐχάς τε καὶ λατρείας, ὅθεν δὴ καθαρμῶν τε καὶ τελετῶν τυχοῦσα ἐξάντη ἐποίησε τὸν [ἑαυτῆς] ἔχοντα πρός τε τὸν παρόντα καὶ τὸν ἔπειτα χρόνον,

Moreover, when diseases and the greatest troubles have been visited upon certain families through some ancient guilt, madness has entered in and by oracular power has found a way of release for those in need, taking refuge in prayers and the service of the gods, and so, by purifications and sacred rites, he who has this madness is made safe for the present and the after time, and for him who is rightly possessed of madness a release from present ills is found.

Ballériaux believes that telestic madness is the kind of Dionysiac madness that hits télestes, in his words “devins-exorcistes” that freed families hit by divine punishment for a past fault inherited from an ancestor (Ballériaux 1990, pp. 42-43). According to the French scholar télestes were the same manteis and agyrtais that Plato mentions in , diviners that could free from ancient unpunished wrongs thanks to a god-inspired madness, the telestic madness.

[…] τότε νόμος ταύτην μὴ φυτεῦσαι εἰς μηδεμίαν θήρειον φύσιν ἐν τῇ πρώτῃ γενέσει, ἀλλὰ τὴν μὲν πλεῖστα ἰδοῦσαν εἰς γονὴν ἀνδρὸς γενησομένου φιλοσόφου ἢ φιλοκάλου ἢ μουσικοῦ τινος καὶ ἐρωτικοῦ, τὴν δὲ δευτέραν εἰς βασιλέως ἐννόμου ἢ πολεμικοῦ καὶ ἀρχικοῦ, τρίτην εἰς πολιτικοῦ ἤ τινος οἰκονομικοῦ ἢ χρηματιστικοῦ, τετάρτην εἰς φιλοπόνου <ἢ> γυμναστικοῦ ἢ περὶ σώματος ἴασίν τινος ἐσομένου, πέμπτην μαντικὸν βίον ἤ τινα τελεστικὸν ἕξουσαν· ἕκτῃ ποιητικὸς ἢ τῶν περὶ μίμησίν τις ἄλλος ἁρμόσει, ἑβδόμῃ δημιουργικὸς ἢ γεωργικός, ὀγδόῃ σοφιστικὸς ἢ δημοκοπικός, ἐνάτῃ τυραννικός.

[…] then it is the law that this soul shall never pass into any beast at its first birth, but the soul that has seen the most shall enter into the birth of a man who is to be a philosopher or a lover of beauty, or one of a musical or loving nature, and the second soul into that of a lawful king or a warlike ruler, and the third into that of a politician or a man of business or a financier, the fourth into that of a hardworking gymnast or one who will be concerned with the cure of the body, and the fifth will lead the life of a prophet or someone who conducts mystic rites; to the sixth, a poet or some other imitative artist will be united, to the seventh, a craftsman or a husbandman, to the eighth, a sophist or a demagogue, to the ninth, a tyrant.

Τῆς δὲ θείας τεττάρων θεῶν τέτταρα μέρη διελόμενοι, μαντικὴν μὲν ἐπίπνοιαν Ἀπόλλωνος θέντες, Διονύσου δὲ τελεστικήν, Μουσῶν δ’ αὖ ποιητικήν, τετάρτην δὲ Ἀφροδίτης καὶ Ἔρωτος, ἐρωτικὴν μανίαν ἐφήσαμέν τε ἀρίστην εἶναι,

And we made four divisions of the divine madness, ascribing them to four gods, saying that prophecy was inspired by Apollo, the mystic madness by Dionysus, the poetic by the Muses, and the madness of love, inspired by Aphrodite and Eros, we said was the best.

2) After Plato it is used in II and III centuries CE by Plutarchus and Aelianus as an adjective with the meaning of “mystical”.

ἐδόκει δέ τις εἶναι θεοφιλὴς καὶ σοφὸς περὶ τὰ θεῖα τὴν ἐνθουσιαστικὴν καὶ τελεστικὴν σοφίαν, διὸ καὶ παῖδα νύμφης ὄνομα Βάλτης καὶ Κούρητα νέον αὐτὸν οἱ τότε ἄνθρωποι προσηγόρευον.

He was reputed to be a man beloved of the gods, and endowed with a mystical and heaven-sent wisdom in religious matters.

καρδίαν δὲ οὐκ ἂν φάγοι ποτὲ ἱέραξ, τελεστικὸν δήπου δρῶν καὶ μυστικὸν ἐκεῖνος τοῦτο.

But a falcon will never eat the heart, thereby presumably fulfilling some mystic rite.

3) In the second half of the II century CE we find for the first time τελεστική in the noun form. It is attested in Maximus of Tyre, who in the 38th discourse speaks about the divine nature of Virtue. To demonstrate the divine origin of human virtue he takes as examples things that although inferior than virtue are still inspired by divine nature. One of these lesser arts is τελεστική, that together with μαντική, ποιητική, καθάρσεις and χρησμῳδίας, compose a list of things that Maximus considers established as coming from divine inspiration. By stating this hierarchy, the author wants to demonstrate the divine origin of Virtue, which being better than those things has to come from divine doing too. “You should be prepared to find that, if God is able to share out and distribute such things as these, he is far more able and willing to distribute Virtue.” (Trapp 1997, p. 303). In the joint citation of mantic, telestic and poetic arts, Maximus may remember the passage from Plato’s , even if here he does not mention erotic mania and adds “purifications and prophecies” (Trapp 1997, p. 302).

‘Ὦ σχέτλιε, τί ταῦτα ἐρεῖς, ὃς ἡγεῖ τὸ καλλιστεῦον τῶν ἀνθρωπίνων ἀγαθῶν παρὰ μὲν ἀνθρώπου τέχνης τάχιστα ἥξειν, ἐκ δὲ θείας ἀρετῆς ἀπορώτατα; Καίτοι μαντικήν, καὶ τελεστικήν, καὶ ποιητικήν, καὶ καθάρ- σεις, καὶ χρησμῳδίας, ξυλλήβδην ἅπαντα, οὐκ ἂν εἴποις ἀντάξια εἶναι τῆς ἀρετῆς·

'You fool, what nonsense is this? Do you really think that the finest of human goods can instantaneously be derived from human science, but only with the greatest difficulty from divine Virtue? Yet at the same time you would not be prepared to say that the arts of the seer and the priest and the poet, and purifications and prophecies, were equal in worth to Virtue, even taken all together.

4) In V CE neoplatonic authors Hermias and Proclus speak of τελεστική as connected with theurgy, defined by Dodds as a branch of τελεστική “applied to a religious purpose and resting on a supposed revelation of a religious character” (Dodds 1951, p. 291) or by Proclus’ words “all the operation of divine possession” (; Dodds, ib.) Dodds considers τελεστική as being one of two rituals, the other being the temporary incarnation of a god in a human being, a medium. τελεστική is defined by Dodds as “the consecrating and animating of τελεστική statues in order to obtain oracles from them” (Dodds 1951, p. 292). This ritual that consecrates statues, making them move under divine possession would have originated in Egypt (Dodds 1951, p. 293). Other authors don’t agree with the scholar and think τελεστική could come from traditional Greek religious rituals, as there are a lot of similar examples of moving statues in Greek literature (Boyancé 1955). The original source is believed to be either the Chaldean oracles, the orphic cults or the Eleusinian mysteries, but scholars are not unanimous on this.

Tελεστική is based on the concept of συμπάθεια, that implies a web of references linking image with original (i.e. the material world with the divine world). This way marks (χαρακτήρης), objects such as plants or minerals, and even animals become symbols (σύμβολα) and carry in themselves a bit of divine essence, each type of plant, animal or mineral corresponding to a different god. From this perspective τελεστική could be defined as a religious ritual that, using material symbols and marks, calls, through the power of συμπάθεια, the divine essence inside an inanimate receptacle, ὑποδοχή, in most cases these receptacles are statues that come alive and move or give oracles.

However some scholars believe τελεστική to have a double meaning: on one hand the pragmatic ritual, on the other hand it would be an intellectual concept to be understood on a philosophical level. According to Van Liefferinge (1999, p. 94) Proclus was the first to coin the term τελεστικήτέχνη and to give it the specific meaning of consecrating statues, even if we find it also in Hermias (). However in neoplatonic authors τελεστική can also mean something higher, a way for the soul to rise and return among the divine world from which it fell. This would mean that τελεστική entails in general an action of divine power and agency, that can provide movement in both directions, upwards, when the soul rises to the gods, and downwards, when humans summon divine presence through marks and objects that share συμπάθεια with the original (Van Liefferinge 1999).

According to Ballériaux (1989, p. 23) in Hermias’ commentary on the Phaedrus the role of τελεστική goes beyond the ritual aspect of consecrating and animating statues. Hermias’ commentary comes from a lecture of the neoplatonic philosopher Syrianus, Hermias and Proclus’ teacher. Ballériaux believes that τελεστική as consecration of statues is only one of the possible enthusiasms, i.e. the divine possession of the soul. These different possessions are described at , after which Hermias describes the last enthusiasm, the possession of statues ().

According to Shepperd (1982) too, Proclus thinks about different types of τελεστική: a pragmatic ritual that enables men to come in contact with the divine even if only for a fleeting moment, and, on a higher level a philosophical union of soul and divine essence (see ).

The term is still used in IX CE by another neoplatonic philosopher, Psellus.

Discussion / interpretation

Discussion / interpretation

τελεστική, ritual practice one uses to induce the presence of a certain god inside an inanimate object used as a receptacle (ὑποδοχή), often a statue of said god. Usually other objects, like plants or minerals, and marks(χαρακτήρης), engraved or written, were used to strengthen the bond between god and receptacle. It is also considered a religious and philosophical concept, first attested in Plato and later resumed by the Neoplatonic authors. Interpreted by modern scholars as either linked to theurgy, if not even one of its synonyms.

First attested in Plato, with the meaning of mystic madness linked to the god Dionysus, τελεστική is then used in the late hellenistic period when the meaning becomes linked with theurgy. It would entail the “consecrating and animating of magic statues in order to obtain oracles from them.” (Dodds 1951). This term is later used by Maximus of Tyre in II CE and by neoplatonic authors, like Hermias and Proclus in V CE, and Psellus in XI CE. Dodds considers τελεστική as one of the rituals that use theurgy intended as a magic power that concerns divine possessions. Other authors believe τελεστική to be an ancient rite engrained in the traditional Greek religion that only later, in neoplatonic texts, is associated with theurgy, and becomes a way to reunite the soul with the divine (Boyancé 1955; Van Liefferinge 1999).
